\section{Regression Results}
\label{sec:regression}
%=============================================================

\subsection*{Overview}

The dependent variable in all regressions is
\(\ln p_{fjct}\), the log outsourcing purchase unit price (CNY per unit)
paid by firm \(f\) for product \(j\) (9-digit code) in city \(c\) in year \(t\) (2017).
Three groups of explanatory variables are considered.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Firm-level variables (\(x\)):}
    \(\ln\text{Output}_{ft}\) (log total VAT sales, size proxy);
    \(\ln K_{ft}\) (log total assets, sourced from the tax-filing bridge,
    approximately 62.3\% coverage; see Section~\ref{sec:bridge});
    \(n^{\text{prod}}_{ft}\) (number of product codes with positive net output).

  \item \textbf{Market-level variables (\(z\)):}
    \(\ln n^{B}_{jct}\) (log number of buyers of product \(j\) in city \(c\),
    universe coverage);
    \(\ln n^{S}_{jct}\) (log number of sellers, universe);
    \(\ln q^{m}_{jct}\) (log total market purchase quantity, universe);
    \(\ln \bar{p}^{m}_{jct}\) (log leave-one-out market average price: the
    ratio of total city--product purchase value to total quantity,
    \emph{excluding the firm's own purchases}, which removes the mechanical
    correlation between the dependent variable and this control).

  \item \textbf{Product-similarity variables (\(s\)):}
    \(S_{mj}\) (\texttt{input\_similarity}, cosine similarity of the input
    structure between the firm's core product \(m\) and the outsourced product
    \(j\)); \(C_{mj}\) (\texttt{output\_similarity}, output-structure
    similarity). Both vary at the firm\(\times\)product level.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Sample.}
The sample consists of 3,410 firms randomly drawn from the VAT database
(a time-saving compromise; will be expanded to the full sample in later work).
The sample passes through the following filtering steps:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,nosep]
  \item \textbf{Buy-side filter}: 3,376 of the 3,410 firms have at least one
    valid 9-digit product code in the purchase records (after bianma filter).
  \item \textbf{Outsourcing definition}: an outsourcing product requires the
    \emph{same} firm to appear on \emph{both} the buy and sell side for the
    \emph{same} product code. Only 2,108 firms satisfy this condition;
    these constitute \texttt{invoice\_panel.dta}.
  \item \textbf{Intermediary removal}: 233 firms with outsourcing ratio
    \(>90\%\) (\texttt{is\_intermediary == 1}) are dropped, leaving
    \textbf{1,875 firms} in the final regression panel
    (\texttt{reg\_panel.dta}).
\end{itemize}
Tables~T1, T2, T4, and T5 require \(\ln K_{ft}\) and therefore have
\(N \approx 29{,}000\) (62.3\% coverage); Tables~T3 and T6 do not require
capital data and have \(N \approx 45{,}956\).

\paragraph{Product portfolio.}
Among the 3,376 buy-side firms, each purchases on average approximately 125
distinct product types (9-digit codes; 425,713 purchase records
\(\div\) 3,376 \(\approx\) 126).
Of these 3,376 firms, 2,108 (62.5\%) have at least one product that appears
on both their buy and sell sides (the outsourcing condition).
These 2,108 outsourcing firms have on average 28.2 outsourcing product types
(mean 28.2, median 3; the distribution is highly right-skewed with a maximum
of 925 products).
In the final regression panel (\texttt{reg\_panel.dta}, 1,875 firms after
dropping intermediaries), the mean is 25.0 outsourcing products per firm
(median 3).

\paragraph{Fixed effects and identification.}
All FE specifications include firm (\(\delta_f\)), product (\(\delta_j\), 9-digit
code), and/or city (\(\delta_c\), prefecture) fixed effects; standard errors are
clustered at the firm level.
Because the data cover a single cross-section (2017), firm-level variables
(\(\ln\text{Output}\), \(\ln K\), \(n^{\text{prod}}\)) are perfectly
collinear with the firm FE and are identified only in OLS specifications.
Similarity variables (\(S_{mj}\), \(C_{mj}\)) vary at the firm\(\times\)product
level and remain identified under firm FE.
Market variables vary at the product\(\times\)city level and remain identified
under firm FE.

\begin{equation}
  \ln p_{fjct}
  = \gamma_1 \ln \text{Output}_{ft}

%-------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{T6: Interactions --- Firm Size \texorpdfstring{$\times$}{x} Market Conditions}
\label{sec:t6}
%-------------------------------------------------------------

To explore heterogeneous price-setting, all specifications include firm, product,
and city FE, and the main effect of \(\ln\text{Output}\) is absorbed by Firm FE.
The equation is
\begin{equation}
  \ln p_{fjct}
  = \boldsymbol{\lambda}' \mathbf{z}_{jct}
  + \sum_{k} \mu_k \bigl(\ln \text{Output}_{ft} \times z^{k}_{jct}\bigr)
  + \delta_f + \delta_j + \delta_c
  + \varepsilon_{fjct}.
  \label{eq:t6}
\end{equation}

\subsection*{Core Python Processing Steps}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.8em, label=\arabic*.]

  \item \textbf{Product code standardization.}
    Raw codes are right-padded to 19 digits; the first 9 digits are retained as
    the standardized product code. Only the 2,778 valid codes from the national
    product catalog are kept.

  \item \textbf{Outsourcing identification.}
    A firm-product pair is classified as an outsourcing transaction if and only
    if the firm appears in \emph{both} \texttt{firm\_buy} and
    \texttt{firm\_sell} for the same product code (i.e., the firm both purchases
    and sells the same product), with
    \(\min(\text{purchase quantity},\, \text{sales quantity}) > 0\).

  \item \textbf{City merge.}
    Because the purchase and sale files contain no geographic identifier,
    \texttt{firm\_city} is joined to both files on firm ID to assign each
    transaction to a prefecture.

  \item \textbf{Market condition construction.}
    Universe views \texttt{city\_buy} and \texttt{city\_sell} are aggregated at
    the product\(\times\)city level to produce \(n^{B}_{jct}\),
    \(n^{S}_{jct}\), \(q^{m}_{jct}\), and \(\bar{p}^{m}_{jct}\).
    The market average price \(\bar{p}^{m}_{jct}\) is constructed on a
    leave-one-out basis: each firm's own purchase value and quantity are
    subtracted from the city--product totals before taking the ratio, so the
    control does not mechanically contain the firm's own price (firms that are
    the sole buyer are set to missing).
    These aggregates are \emph{not} restricted to sample firms, ensuring that
    the market-thickness measures capture the full competitive environment.

  \item \textbf{Intermediary removal.}
    Firms flagged as intermediaries (\texttt{is\_intermediary == 1};
    outsourcing ratio \(>90\%\)) are dropped from the 2,108-firm outsourcing
    panel. This removes 233 firms, leaving \textbf{1,875 firms} in the final
    regression panel (\texttt{reg\_panel.dta}).

\end{enumerate}

\section{Bridge Data Source and Matching Rate}
\label{sec:bridge}
%=============================================================

\subsection*{Purpose}

The VAT invoice database contains transaction-level information but no firm
financial data. To include \(\ln K_{ft}\) (log total assets) as a regressor in
Tables~T1, T2, T4, and T5, firm identifiers are bridged to a separate tax-filing
dataset that records balance-sheet information.

\subsection*{Bridge Path}

\[
  \underbrace{\text{Firm ID (VAT, \texttt{cid})}}_{\text{VAT database}}
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  \underbrace{\text{Tax-filing matched file}}_{\text{bridge key}}
  \;\longrightarrow\;
  \underbrace{K_{ft}\;(\text{total assets, double precision})}_{\text{tax filing data}}
\]
